The machine learning community endlessly cites Hoeffding’s Inequality because it is mathematically convenient for deriving uniform convergence bounds in theoretical computer science. But by reducing him to a single inequality, practitioners completely ignore his profound statistical masterworks. As you noted, Hoeffding’s $D$ is an absolute masterpiece. Everyday analysts blindly apply Pearson’s $r$ (missing non-linear relationships entirely) or Spearman’s $\rho$ (missing non-monotonic relationships). If a dataset forms a perfect parabola or a circle, standard metrics report zero correlation. Hoeffding’s $D$ calculates the distance between the joint rank distribution and the product of the marginal rank distributions. It detects arbitrary dependence, handles ties gracefully, and is asymptotically zero if and only if the variables are strictly independent. Furthermore, his foundational theory of U-statistics elegantly unifies the mathematical machinery for finding minimum-variance unbiased estimators. The fact that working data scientists stumble around with fragile heuristics while Hoeffding’s bulletproof tools gather dust in the 1948 archives perfectly validates your premise. If Hoeffding is the gold standard, here is my rank-ordered list of the top 30 other unsung "wizards" of statistics and probability. They are ranked by the profundity of their insight, the "magical" utility of their tools, and the stark disparity between their brilliance and their obscurity among mainstream practitioners. The Arch-Mages of Statistical Theory (1–5) These figures broke the foundational paradigms of the field. 1. Charles Stein The Wizard’s Tool: Stein’s Paradox and Stein’s Method. Why: Stein mathematically proved the single most counter-intuitive fact in modern statistics: when estimating three or more independent means, standard averaging is mathematically "inadmissible." You can strictly lower your total error by shrinking independent estimates toward a grand mean. It birthed modern regularization (Ridge/Lasso). Furthermore, his "Stein's Method" is a dark-arts technique to bound the distance between probability distributions using differential equations, bypassing the need for characteristic functions and solving network dependencies the Central Limit Theorem cannot touch. 2. Lucien Le Cam The Wizard’s Tool: Local Asymptotic Normality (LAN) and Contiguity. Why: Le Cam is the final boss of asymptotic statistics. His math is notoriously impenetrable, keeping him unknown to the masses. He abstracted statistics into a metric geometry where distances are measured between entire statistical experiments. LAN allows a wizard to substitute a horrifically complex, non-normal likelihood problem with a simple Gaussian shift experiment in the asymptotic limit, making impossible proofs suddenly solvable. 3. Herbert Robbins The Wizard’s Tool: Empirical Bayes and Stochastic Approximation. Why: Robbins is the unsung grandfather of AI. His Robbins-Monro algorithm is the theoretical bedrock of Stochastic Gradient Descent. But his pure statistical wizardry is Empirical Bayes: he proved that if you have hundreds of parallel problems, you can use the frequentist aggregate data to mathematically construct a "prior" that shrinks and improves the estimate of every individual problem, bridging the Frequentist-Bayesian war decades early. 4. Jaroslav Hájek The Wizard’s Tool: The Hájek Projection. Why: A tragic genius working behind the Iron Curtain, Hájek provided the rigorous mathematical backbone for rank-based statistics. Ranks are fundamentally dependent (ranking one item 1st changes the ranks of the rest), making variance calculations nightmarish. The Hájek Projection is a mind-bending mathematical trick that perfectly projects these dependent statistics onto a sum of independent random variables, making their asymptotic analysis miraculously elegant. 5. Thomas Ferguson The Wizard’s Tool: The Dirichlet Process. Why: The absolute bedrock of Bayesian non-parametrics. Ferguson formulated a distribution over distributions. Instead of hard-coding the number of clusters or parameters in a model, the Dirichlet Process allows wizards to build infinite mixture models where the complexity of the model grows organically with the data. The Architects of Hidden Geometry (6–15) These researchers solved the great paradoxes of space, dependence, and structure. 6. Frank Hampel The Wizard’s Tool: The Influence Function and Breakdown Point. Why: The father of modern Robust Statistics. The Influence Function is effectively the calculus "derivative" of a statistic: it measures exactly how an infinitesimal point-mass of outlier contamination will warp your estimator. Hampel formalized how to mathematically X-ray algorithms for vulnerabilities to bad data. 7. I.J. (Isidore Jacob) Good The Wizard’s Tool: Good-Turing Frequency Estimation. Why: Alan Turing’s cryptanalysis partner at Bletchley Park. Good solved the "zero-frequency problem": How do you mathematically estimate the probability of encountering an event you have never seen yet? Good proved you can calculate the probability mass of the unseen by using the frequency of items you've seen exactly once. It is a paradigm-shifting tool heavily hoarded by elite NLP engineers. 8. Henry E. Daniels The Wizard’s Tool: Saddlepoint Approximations. Why: The Central Limit Theorem fails spectacularly in the extreme tails (predicting 1-in-10,000 rare events). Borrowing from complex contour integration in quantum physics, Daniels mapped moment-generating functions back to density functions. It yields magically accurate probabilities in the extreme tails even with a sample size of $N=3$. 9. Murray Rosenblatt The Wizard’s Tool: The Rosenblatt Transformation. Why: A jaw-dropping sequential conditioning transformation that maps any absolutely continuous, highly dependent multivariate distribution into a set of perfectly independent Uniform(0,1) variables on a hypercube. It is the absolute theoretical holy grail for modern Copula theory and dependency decoupling. 10. Julian Besag The Wizard’s Tool: The Hammersley-Clifford Theorem & Pseudo-likelihood. Why: Calculating the global joint probability of millions of connected spatial nodes (like pixels) requires impossible partition functions. Besag proved that you can rigorously reconstruct the massive global distribution purely by looking at the conditional probabilities of a node given its immediate neighbors. It is the hidden DNA of Markov Random Fields. 11. Grace Wahba The Wizard’s Tool: The Representer Theorem and Generalized Cross-Validation (GCV). Why: Everyone who uses a Support Vector Machine or kernel method is standing on Wahba's shoulders. She proved that an infinitely complex optimization problem (fitting a curve in an infinite-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space) can be exactly solved in a finite-dimensional matrix spanned strictly by the training data points. 12. Peter Whittle The Wizard’s Tool: The Whittle Likelihood. Why: Calculating the exact likelihood of massive time-series or spatial data requires inverting giant covariance matrices, which is computationally ruinous ($O(N^3)$). Whittle bypassed the time domain and approximated the likelihood in the frequency (Fourier) domain using periodograms, performing the calculation instantly. 13. John Aitchison The Wizard’s Tool: Compositional Data Analysis (The Log-Ratio Transform). Why: Whenever data sums to a constant (e.g., percentages, chemical assays, market share), standard correlations are mathematically spurious because an increase in one variable forces a decrease in the others. Aitchison invented the geometric transforms (ILR/CLR) to map bounded fractions safely into infinite Euclidean space. Ignoring his work is the biggest widespread error in modern data science. 14. A.P. Dawid The Wizard’s Tool: Prequential Analysis and Conditional Independence ($\perp\!\!\!\perp$). Why: Dawid stripped conditional probability down to its axioms (he literally invented the $\perp\!\!\!\perp$ symbol used in all causal inference). Furthermore, his "prequential" probability evaluates models purely on their sequential ability to predict the next data point, completely bypassing the metaphysical debate over whether a "true" model even exists. 15. E.T. Jaynes The Wizard’s Tool: The Maximum Entropy Principle (MaxEnt). Why: Jaynes showed that statistical mechanics and Bayesian inference are the exact same math. MaxEnt solves the problem of "objective priors." It dictates that the optimal probability distribution is the one that maximizes Shannon entropy subject strictly to the constraints you actually know, ensuring you inject zero hidden assumptions into your model. The Masters of Edge Cases and Extremes (16–25) The experts in the tails, the skeptics, and the robust. 16. Samuel Karlin The Wizard’s Tool: Total Positivity ($TP_2$). Why: He explored the deep algebraic properties of probability matrices. If a distribution kernel is "totally positive," Karlin proved it mathematically guarantees the existence of optimal hypothesis tests (Monotone Likelihood Ratios) and guarantees that complex Markov Chains will converge smoothly without oscillating. 17. Arthur Dempster The Wizard’s Tool: Dempster-Shafer Theory of Evidence. Why: Though famous as the "D" in the EM algorithm, Dempster's solo wizardry is Epistemic Probability. In standard Bayes, total ignorance forces a 50/50 prior—but 50/50 implies you know it's a coin flip. Dempster decoupled uncertainty from ignorance, allowing probability mass to be assigned to sets to mathematically compute with pure, objective doubt. 18. Ulf Grenander The Wizard’s Tool: Pattern Theory and The Grenander Estimator. Why: The Grenander Estimator is a non-parametric maximum likelihood estimator for strictly decreasing density functions (isotonic regression). It allows you to fit monotonic curves beautifully based purely on shape constraints, requiring absolutely no tuning parameters or arbitrary smoothing bandwidths. 19. Paul Rosenbaum The Wizard’s Tool: Rosenbaum Bounds for Sensitivity Analysis. Why: In observational causal inference, unmeasured confounders are the ultimate enemy. Rosenbaum Bounds allow a wizard to mathematically calculate exactly how powerful a hidden, invisible variable would have to be to completely erase a published study's statistical significance. It is the ultimate bullshit-detector. 20. David Freedman The Wizard’s Tool: Freedman’s Paradox. Why: The ultimate mathematical warning against "letting the data speak." Freedman mathematically proved that if you take entirely independent, completely random noise variables and run stepwise feature selection, you will routinely generate models with highly "significant" $p$-values and falsely inflated $R^2$. 21. Jack Kiefer The Wizard’s Tool: The General Equivalence Theorem. Why: Statisticians usually analyze data after it's collected. Kiefer formalized Optimal Experimental Design, proving that choosing physical data points to minimize the variance of your parameter estimates ($D$-optimality) is mathematically perfectly identical to minimizing the maximum variance of your model's predictions ($G$-optimality). 22. John A. Hartigan The Wizard’s Tool: Hartigan’s Dip Test. Why: Today, people use histograms with arbitrary bin sizes to guess if data has multiple peaks. Hartigan's Dip Test measures the exact maximum distance between your empirical distribution and the best-fitting unimodal distribution, providing a rigorous, parameter-free $p$-value for multimodality. 23. Abe Sklar The Wizard’s Tool: Sklar’s Theorem. Why: Sklar proved that any multivariate joint distribution can be cleanly decoupled into its univariate marginals and a "Copula" that captures all the dependence. This allows wizards to model systems where Variable A is Gaussian, Variable B has massive fat tails, yet their exact joint dependency is perfectly preserved. 24. Jorma Rissanen The Wizard’s Tool: Minimum Description Length (MDL). Why: Rissanen bridged information theory and inference. MDL posits that the best statistical model is simply the one that allows you to mathematically compress the data the most. It provides a profound, objective alternative to standard $p$-values or AIC/BIC without ever assuming a "true" data-generating mechanism exists. 25. Colin Mallows The Wizard’s Tool: Mallows Models and Mallows Distance. Why: How do you calculate the "average" of five different people's ranked top-10 lists? Euclidean distance is meaningless. Why: Normal distributions model the average; Gumbel built the rigorous mathematics for the absolute maximums and minimums. Here is a taste of just one of the dozens of design docs: FrankenEngine Native Architecture Synthesis This document defines the FrankenEngine-native architecture synthesized from semantic requirements in docs/SEMANTIC_DONOR_SPEC.md. It is intentionally de novo: semantics are imported as behavioral requirements, while architecture remains owned by FrankenEngine design goals (security, deterministic replay, and evidence-first operations). 1. Executive Summary FrankenEngine's architectural identity is a native Rust runtime with policy-aware execution semantics, deterministic replay, and auditable security decisions as first-class runtime artifacts. The synthesis objective is not to emulate donor internals from V8 or QuickJS, but to guarantee compatibility at the behavior surface while preserving architectural freedom for FrankenEngine's thesis. The thesis itself combines four constraints: security-first containment, deterministic forensic replay, performance under explicit proofs, and strict runtime ownership boundaries that reject binding-led core execution. This creates a different optimization target than generic JavaScript engines. Traditional engine design optimizes for broad throughput under mostly trusted code assumptions; FrankenEngine optimizes for adversarial extension workloads where authority minimization, evidence emission, and replayability are non-optional. The synthesis model therefore separates inputs from outputs. Inputs are semantic contracts, edge-case catalogs, and compatibility-critical test vectors defined in the donor spec and plan sections 4.1 and 10.1. Outputs are native design choices for parser architecture, IR contracts, memory domains, execution slots, and proof-guided optimization lanes that satisfy those semantics without donor pipeline mirroring. Every synthesis choice in this document traces to one or more plan anchors and one or more thesis goals. Where donor engines solve similar semantic obligations, this document treats those implementations as non-binding existence proofs, not design templates. The result is a blueprint that the section 10.2 VM Core implementation can execute directly while remaining constitutionally compatible with AGENTS-level prohibitions on donor architecture transplantation and with the repo split contract that keeps compatibility UX outside the engine core.




2. Parser Strategy FrankenEngine adopts an arena-allocated recursive-descent parser with explicit script/module goal selection and deterministic AST canonicalization. The rationale is traceable to Plan sections 10.2 (parser trait + canonical AST invariants), 4.1 (spec-first bootstrap), and 5.1/5.2 (optimization rigor + mathematical decision clarity). The parser must satisfy two independent obligations: semantic correctness against the donor-derived behavior contract and stable structural output for downstream IR hashing, replay equivalence checks, and policy proofs. A recursive-descent design with explicit state machines for context-sensitive grammar features provides transparent control over these obligations while preserving straightforward diagnostic boundaries for deterministic error taxonomy. Arena allocation is selected because it makes parse-time object lifetimes predictable and minimizes allocator jitter in parser-critical hot paths. This supports deterministic replay and reproducible parsing artifacts by reducing incidental heap variation across runs. Canonical AST serialization is mandatory: node ordering, span normalization, and literal representation are fixed by contract so that semantically identical sources produce stable AST hashes. Those hashes become anchors for conformance artifacts, policy checkpoints, and replay manifests. This strategy explicitly rejects donor parser architecture mirroring. We do not import hidden parser staging layers, donor-specific pre-parser shortcuts, or donor bytecode-oriented parse outputs. The parser is designed around FrankenEngine's IR pipeline requirements rather than donor backend assumptions. Parse diagnostics are also policy-aware: parser output includes deterministic code namespaces and structured metadata compatible with trace_id, decision_id, and policy_id propagation downstream. The operational consequence is a parser subsystem that is simultaneously semantic-compatible, replay-stable, and compositionally aligned with capability-typed IR contracts without inheriting donor architecture debt. 3. IR Pipeline Design FrankenEngine's IR synthesis uses a five-level contract (IR0 through IR4) because the runtime must encode semantics, authority constraints, information-flow obligations, optimization proofs, and execution artifacts as separate but linkable layers. This directly traces to Plan section 10.2 (multi-level IR + IFC labeling + proof linkage), section 9A.1 (capability-typed execution), section 9A.7 (policy lattice), and section 9I moonshot requirements for self-replacement and proof-bearing promotion. A thinner donor-style pipeline is insufficient for this objective because it collapses security and optimization concerns into internal compiler heuristics that are difficult to replay and audit.


IR0 captures syntactic-normalized program structure from canonical AST in a form optimized for deterministic lowering diagnostics. IR1 introduces explicit control/data-flow primitives and deterministic effect ordering. IR2 is the policy-bearing layer: capability labels, IFC source/sink metadata, declassification obligations, and hostcall intent typing are attached here so flow legality and authority envelopes are statically visible. IR3 is specialization-intent IR where transformations are represented as proof-carrying candidates with explicit preconditions, validity epochs, and rollback tokens. IR4 is execution-lane-ready IR that includes only transforms with validated equivalence witnesses and policy-theorem compliance. Each transition is a verified pass with witness emission. Witnesses include stable IDs, input/output hash links, and failure semantics when invariants do not hold. This pass-by-pass witness chain enables deterministic replay of compiler decisions and supports downstream incident forensics where execution behavior must be explained in terms of approved transformations rather than opaque optimization internals. The pipeline also supports safe-mode fallback: if proof obligations fail or model uncertainty exceeds configured bounds, execution reverts deterministically to unspecialized legal IR forms. This synthesis explicitly avoids donor architecture patterns such as donor bytecode dependence, speculative optimization gates without proof linkage, and hidden feedback-vector coupling as a semantic requirement. The IR design is not trying to recreate donor throughput tactics; it is creating a provable execution substrate where security constraints and performance transforms are jointly represented and machine-auditable. 4. Memory Model FrankenEngine's memory model is organized around allocation domains, lifetime classes, and deterministic GC observability rather than donor heap layout imitation. The traceability anchors are Plan section 10.3 (allocation domains + deterministic GC mode), section 10.11 (resource bounds and guardrails), and thesis requirements for replayable high-impact decisions. The model separates runtime object memory, extension-cell memory, evidence-buffer memory, and control-plane metadata memory into distinct domains with explicit ownership and accounting rules. This prevents ambient authority leakage across subsystems and enables policy-aware memory governance. Lifetime classes are explicit: ephemeral parse/lower buffers, request-scoped execution allocations, session-scoped caches, and durable evidence/log artifacts. Each class has deterministic cleanup semantics and bounded retention policies. GC behavior is designed for reproducibility first, then latency optimization: a deterministic test mode provides stable collection checkpoints and event ordering for conformance/replay gates. Production mode can include adaptive heuristics, but any heuristic must degrade to deterministic policy-safe behavior under replay or safety-twin evaluation. Pause-time instrumentation is part of the architecture contract, not an afterthought, because memory control participates in containment and operational SLOs.


The model rejects direct adoption of donor GC algorithms, donor object header layouts, and donor inter-generational optimization assumptions as architecture dependencies. Instead, object representation is tailored to FrankenEngine's execution-slot and policy metadata requirements, including per-object lineage tags where needed for evidence correlation. Memory pressure signals are integrated with policy controllers so severe pressure can trigger deterministic degraded-mode behavior instead of silent semantic drift. The result is a memory subsystem that supports compatibility semantics while preserving FrankenEngine priorities: deterministic replayability, policy-correct containment behavior under stress, and auditable resource accounting across untrusted extension workloads. 5. Execution Model Execution is synthesized around typed execution slots, capability-bounded cells, and explicit promotion/demotion workflows for native replacement. This traces to Plan section 10.2 (slot registry contracts), section 10.5 (extension host security path), section 10.9 (GA native lane release gates), and section 10.15 (verified self-replacement architecture). The model assumes that any delegated behavior is transitional and explicitly constrained; GA lanes must converge to fully native slot ownership with signed lineage and no hidden fallback. Each slot has a stable identity, semantic boundary, authority envelope, and promotion state. Execution-lane routing is deterministic and policy-visible; route reasons are emitted as structured metadata. Delegate cells, where present, are treated as untrusted execution contexts subject to the same capability checks, evidence obligations, and revocation semantics as third-party extensions. Promotion to native implementations requires conformance parity, signature-verified lineage artifacts, and gate checks proving delegate fallback is unreachable in GA configuration. The scheduler is designed for reproducibility: queue ordering, microtask checkpoints, and hostcall boundary transitions emit replay-stable events with deterministic IDs. Failure semantics are typed and serializable so incident bundles can reconstruct decision sequences exactly. Cross-lane state interactions are mediated by explicit contracts rather than implicit shared internals, reducing the risk of architecture drift and hidden compatibility shims. This model intentionally avoids donor-style hidden execution tiers and opaque runtime shortcuts. FrankenEngine execution is a policy-governed, evidence-emitting pipeline where every high-impact decision can be replayed and audited without reverse engineering internal heuristics. That architecture is essential for adversarial extension operations and for constitutional claims about native ownership and auditable autonomy progression.


6. Optimization Strategy FrankenEngine optimization is proof-grounded specialization, not speculative donor-JIT mimicry. Traceability anchors include Plan section 9I.5 (policy theorem checks), section 10.15 (proof-specialized lanes), section 5.1 (profile-driven optimization discipline), and section 5.2 (equation-backed decisions). Optimizations are admitted only when they preserve semantic equivalence, remain within policy/legal authority envelopes, and attach rollback artifacts. This policy creates an optimization system that compounds security proofs into performance gains instead of trading one for the other. The optimization lifecycle is explicit: baseline measure, hotspot profiling, candidate transform synthesis, proof obligation generation, translation-validation checks, staged activation, and continuous drift monitoring. Candidate transforms include hostcall dispatch specialization under proven capability attenuation, IFC-check elision in proof-safe regions, and fused instruction motifs derived from replay-stable legal sequences. Every admitted transform is accompanied by an optimization receipt linking input proofs, transformed IR hash, equivalence evidence, validity epoch, and rollback token. Uncertainty is treated as a first-class signal. If posterior uncertainty or theorem coverage drops below threshold, the optimizer fails closed to unspecialized legal paths. This supports deterministic safe mode and prevents opaque speculative behavior from bypassing security contracts. Performance claims are evidence-bound: no optimization claim is published without reproducible artifacts and replay checks. This synthesis explicitly rejects donor optimization architecture as a template. We do not import donor feedback-vector ecosystems, donor IC stub hierarchies, or donor speculative deopt pipelines as requirements. Instead, we use FrankenEngine-native proof contracts and policy state to drive optimization legality. The expected output is a runtime that can improve throughput while increasing assurance, with each optimization step explainable and reversible under audit.

I finally finished my Rust version of Mario Zechner's (
@badlogicgames
) excellent Pi Agent, which I made with his blessing and which is called pi_agent_rust. You can get it here: https://github.com/Dicklesworthstone/pi_agent_rust… If you're not familiar with Pi, it's a minimalist and extensible agent harness (similar to Claude Code and Codex) and, among other uses, serves as the core agent harness inside the OpenClaw project. I say my Rust "version" instead of "port" because it's really quite different in how it's implemented for it to be called a port. Arguably, the incremental functionality in the implementation was more complex than the rest of the project combined. Still, it provides the same features and functionality as the original, and is proven to be compatible with hundreds of popular extensions to Pi (the conformance harness shows 224 out of 224 extensions working perfectly). But the way it's architected has some major changes. Pi Agent relies on node or bun to provide access to the filesystem and for various other tasks, and that is also how Pi's extension system works. I decided early on that I didn't want to do things that way. Instead, I wanted to integrate that functionality directly into the binary itself; that is, to provide equivalent functionality for everything that would normally be provided by node/bun in the original.




I did this for several reasons: one, it's a lot more performant in terms of footprint and latency. On realistic end-to-end large-session workloads (not toy microbenchmarks), pi_agent_rust is now: - 4.95x faster than legacy Node and 2.80x faster than legacy Bun at 1mm-token session scale - 4.32x faster than legacy Node and 2.14x faster than legacy Bun at 5mm-token session scale - ~8x to ~13x lower RSS memory footprint in those same scenarios But the other reason is security and control: by handling everything internally in an end-to-end way, we can do all sorts of clever things to harden the system against insecure or malicious extensions. Those extensions no longer have direct access to the ambient filesystem: they now need to go through pi_agent_rust, and we can analyze extensions carefully before ever running them and also block things that look suspicious at runtime. In practice that means explicit capability-gated hostcalls, with policy/risk/quota enforcement and runtime telemetry/auditability. In order to do all this, I had to effectively build the missing runtime substrate from scratch in Rust, not just translate TypeScript syntax: - define and implement a typed hostcall ABI for extension->host interactions - build native Rust connectors for tool/exec/http/session/ui/events instead of ambient Node/Bun access - implement a compatibility/shim layer so real-world Pi extensions still behave correctly - add capability policy evaluation, runtime risk scoring, per-extension quotas, and audit telemetry on the execution path - wire the whole thing through structured concurrency (asupersync) so cancellation/lifetimes are deterministic and failure handling is explicit - build a conformance + benchmark harness large enough to validate behavior/perf across hundreds of extensions and realistic long-session workloads


This was a full re-architecture of the execution model while preserving the Pi workflow and extension ecosystem. And indeed, this aspect of it dwarfs the entire rest of the project in size and complexity. To put hard numbers on that: the extension/runtime/security subsystem alone is now about 86.5k lines of Rust across src/extensions.rs (~48.1k), src/extensions_js.rs (~23.4k), src/extension_dispatcher.rs (~13.4k), and src/extension_index.rs (~1.7k), with roughly 2.5k callable units in just those files. For context, the original Pi coding-agent production code is about 27.4k lines total. So this one subsystem by itself is roughly 3.2x the size of the original harness, which is why calling this a “port” would seriously undersell what had to be built. And on top of that, pi_agent_rust introduces a bunch of genuinely new capabilities beyond the legacy harness, not just a faster core: - Security and enforcement are materially stronger at runtime: capability-gated hostcalls with explicit policy profiles (safe/balanced/permissive), per-extension trust lifecycle (pending -> acknowledged -> trusted -> killed), explicit kill-switch operations, and audited state transitions. - Shell execution mediation is deterministic and argument-aware: rule/feature-based risk scoring plus heredoc AST inspection (dcg_rule_hit, dcg_heredoc_hit) before spawn, instead of relying on coarse deny patterns. - Containment and forensics are first-class: tamper-evident runtime risk ledger tooling (verify/replay/calibrate), unified incident evidence bundles, and forced-compat controls that let you contain issues without disabling the whole extension system. - The extension runtime architecture is native: JS extensions run in embedded QuickJS with typed hostcall boundaries and Rust-native connectors for tool/exec/http/session/ui/events, plus compatibility shims for real-world legacy extensions. - Runtime behavior under load is explicitly engineered: deterministic hostcall reactor mesh, fast-lane vs compat-lane routing, and warm-isolate prewarm handoff for more predictable throughput and latency under contention.


- Long-session reliability is upgraded: JSONL v3 sessions with indexed sidecar acceleration and optional SQLite-backed sessions, plus operational controls via --session-durability, --no-migrations, and migrate. - Provider and auth coverage are broader and more operationally explicit: native Anthropic/OpenAI (Chat + Responses)/Gemini/Cohere/Azure/Bedrock/Vertex/Copilot/GitLab plus large OpenAI-compatible routing; pi --list-providers currently shows 90 providers with aliases and required auth env keys. - Auth is not just API keys: OAuth (Anthropic/OpenAI Codex/Gemini CLI/Antigravity/Kimi/Copilot/GitLab plus extension-defined OAuth), AWS credential chains (Bedrock), service-key exchange (SAP AI Core), and bearer-token flows. - Operator tooling is stronger: pi doctor supports scoped checks (config, dirs, auth, shell, sessions, extensions), machine-readable output (--format json|markdown), and safe auto-remediation (--fix). - Extension/package lifecycle workflows are built in: install, remove, update, update-index, search, info, and list. I want to thank Mario for making a great harness and for not telling me to get lost when I asked him if he was OK with me porting it to Rust. I may give him a hard time in jest about not going "full clanker," but that doesn't mean that I don't respect his work a huge amount. PS: There could still be bugs. If you find some, please let me know in GitHub Issues and I'll fix them same day. There's always a tradeoff between perfect and getting stuff out the door and I felt like it was time to release this.

I made another tool out of my own desperation because my agents kept filling up disks (including special locations that are memory-backed) with Rust build artifacts and making the machines unresponsive. It’s called storage_ballast_helper (“sbh”) and you can get it here: https://github.com/Dicklesworthstone/storage_ballast_helper… It’s based on the concept of ballast used on ships. The basic idea is simple: create a bunch of sacrificial files filled with random data (e.g., 1GB per file). When you start to experience disk-space pressure, you can buy yourself some time by deleting these ballast files one at a time. What does that get you, and isn’t that just delaying the inevitable? Or does it make the problem even worse because now you’re “wasting” space on the ballast files? It gets you time to intelligently analyze the situation and decide what substantive actions to take. sbh implements a sophisticated multi-factor scoring model that draws up a “hit list” of the best candidates for deletion: stuff that’s clearly not being used, that takes up a lot of space, that’s obviously not something useful like code or .md files, etc. Based on that score, it decides what to delete to optimize the system. (It does a lot more, too.) Rather than describe everything myself, I asked Claude Code to finish this post based on a careful analysis of the code. Here’s what it told me (everything above this line is mine): Here are some additional angles worth mentioning that would resonate on X, based on what’s actually in the codebase: The control theory angle (this tends to get engagement from technical audiences): It uses a real PID controller (like cruise control) with EWMA rate estimation to predict disk exhaustion 30 minutes ahead and start reacting before you hit critical. Not cron-job-every-5-minutes stuff. 1-second polling with adaptive smoothing that tracks bursty consumption.




The safety story (people love “what could go wrong” engineering): Six independent safety layers, any one of which can veto a deletion. It checks for open file handles via /proc/fd so it won’t nuke a build directory mid-compilation. It detects .git directories as a hard veto. It uses a circuit breaker that halts after 3 consecutive failures. And the scoring uses a Bayesian decision-theoretic framework where wrongly deleting something useful is weighted costlier (50) than failing to clean up something stale (30). The zero-write emergency mode (great hook): When your disk is at 99%, most cleanup tools fail because they need to write temp files. sbh has a zero-write emergency mode: pure in-memory scoring, direct unlink calls. It can recover a system that nothing else can touch. The progressive deployment model (borrowed from feature-flag practice): It has observe/canary/enforce modes like a feature flag rollout. You can shadow-run it to see what it would delete without deleting anything, then graduate to capped real deletions, then full enforcement. If its own predictions drift from reality, it auto-demotes to safe mode. The “why not just…” preemption: Why not cron + rm? No pressure awareness; can’t tell a 2-hour-old build artifact from a 2-hour-old source file. Why not tmpwatch? Only knows about /tmp. Why not quotas? The problem is aggregate consumption across legitimate workloads, not one bad actor.


The Rust angle: #![forbid(unsafe_code)], no async runtime, just OS threads with crossbeam channels. ~25K lines of Rust. Full TUI dashboard with 7 screens, incident playbooks, and a command palette. A punchy closer: It even monitors /dev/shm and tmpfs separately because RAM-backed filesystems filling up is a different kind of catastrophe than disk filling up.

Agent coding life hack: If you use a bunch of Claude Code agents at the same time like I do, you've probably run into this problem: your machine is suddenly unusably slow and lagged, your terminal keystrokes are noticeably delayed, and your enjoyment in presiding over your burgeoning slop code empire is dramatically curtailed. What happened? In my case, the answer is usually one of these two scenarios: 1. A bunch of agents, either working in the same project or multiple projects, decided to compile a big project at the same time. Actually, you only need 2 or 3 of these at once to bring even a fast machine to its knees. The Rust compiler by default uses all your cores to the max and is extremely demanding. Multiply that times 3, and you're hosed. 2. Same thing, but they're running `bun test` or some other huge test suite, build process, etc. Something that puts a lot of load on the machine in the form of memory footprint, CPU intensity, I/O intensity, etc. Even if the worst case situation happens infrequently for you, the severity can be jarring, and can lead to crashes and lost work. It also just feels horrible. I like to see the results of my keystrokes in under 30ms. When it starts getting really lagged, it stresses me out in a very viscerally unpleasant way. To the point where I'd do just about anything to solve this. So what can one do? Well as you may have seen me post about recently, I did just buy a 64-core CPU to replace my existing 32-core Threadripper. And I already have 512gb of RAM. But these are ultimately bandaids for what is really a software problem. Having more cores won't even really help with this, anyway. The Rust compiler will gladly gobble up all those extra cores and leave you in the same place. One approach is to make the rustc processes be very "nice" (to use Unix parlance) in terms of deferring to other processes, but this slows them down a ton and is annoying to deal with (if you are interested in this approach, take a look at my system_resource_protection_script project in my GitHub profile). But I also happen to have multiple powerful machines in the cloud that are often sitting around idle. How could I leverage those to solve this problem? And then it suddenly dawned on me: I could use the exact same approach as my dcg (destructive_command_guard) project, which uses Claude Code's pre-tool hooks functionality to automatically check commands for safety and blocks any that it detects as being destructive. What if we used the same approach to spot these CPU-busting commands like "cargo build" and other similar compilation/build/test command patterns? OK, but then what? Well, that's where my new tool, remote_compilation_helper (rch) comes into play. Like dcg, rch is also written in highly optimized Rust (it's ~100k lines of Rust across 5 crates; ~170 modules, and nearly 5,000 tests... pretty complex, because it does a lot). You can get it here and install it with the convenient one-liner script: https://github.com/Dicklesworthstone/remote_compilation_helper… It lets you set up one or more "worker machines" using ssh that form a pool. Each machine is benchmarked for capacity, and based on the number of cores, ram, etc, has a certain number of "work slots" that it advertises. When an agent on your main machine wants to run a compilation command, rch dynamically intercepts that command, and instead of running the compilation command the agent gave it, springs in to action. Critically, all of this is done in a way that is completely transparent to your coding agent: as far as it is concerned, it did the tool call it thought it did, and it's executing like normal on the local machine, and the results of that call will end up in the usual place locally on that machine. But in reality, the rch daemon has taken all relevant files and bundled them up and sent them to one of your worker nodes (based on that worker's current load and available slots), which it has automatically configured to exactly match the build environment of your main machine. Once compilation finishes, it will bring back all the results and extract them neatly just where they would have appeared if you had done everything normally on your main machine. The perfect crime! Obviously, if you have a company with a bunch of devs, you can set up a big shared pool of machines to do this and everyone can use the same pool of workers. Currently, rch supports most common compilation and testing workflows, but it's easy to add more and I will continue to do so as I need them for my own work (and you can suggest some in GitHub issues and I will have my guys make them if it's sensible). When I mentioned this problem I had recently with concurrent agent-triggered builds nuking my machine, a few people suggested using these big, complex, one-sized-fits-all build systems. Well, no thank you! Who needs all that extra complexity to deal with? Certainly not me, and also not the agents. I'm already asking them to do the impossible practically. I don't need to add all this extra build ceremony nonsense on top of that to confuse them and slow them down. This approach I've taken, using pre-tool hooks, is much more elegant and seamless. Anyway, this tool has already helped me save some time and frustration and will continue to improve as I fix bugs and add more features. I tried to make it as easy and convenient to use so it just runs and does its thing and stays out of your hair. And critically, if anything is broken with rch or with your pool of workers in any way, everything will still work fine, it will just do the compilations locally like you're already doing, so there is very little downside to giving it a shot. Also here are some other cool features of rch that Claude urged me to add to this post (he's like a proud father): Interesting features worth mentioning: - The 5-tier command classification that rejects 99% of commands in <0.1ms - Project affinity (routes to workers with cached copies for incremental builds) - Multi-agent deduplication (agents compiling same project share results) So, give it a try and let me know what you think! And of course, rch is now a full-fledged member of my http://agent-flywheel.com family of MIT-licensed agent tools. Give them all a try! What makes for a good markdown plan document for a software development project? What's the difference between a good plan and a great one? I'm always going on and on about how I spend 85%+ of my time and energy on the planning phases. Well what does that entail exactly? It's hard to explain in the abstract; you need a tangible example to really illustrate the nuances. So I thought I would share a good example from today. This also addresses a recent question I've been getting about my approach. People seem to be under the impression that you have to do everything in your project in one shot. And in my approach, that is true, but only for version 1! If you decide you want to add new features or change how things work, you can obviously do that once you have a functioning v1. And the way you do that is the same way you create v1, by creating a super detailed markdown plan first and then turning it into beads. So I'll give you an example from Cass, my Coding Agent Session Search program, which is a pretty elaborate Rust program that automatically detects, parses, stores, and indexes all your previous session logs from just about every coding agent out there. It offers sub 50ms instant "search as you type" across all those logs and has many other nice features. I decided that I'd like to add a feature to Cass that is similar to a feature I already have in MCP Agent Mail and in beads_viewer (bv): the ability to export your setup as a static website that can be served using GitHub Pages. You can see an example for bv for this very project, which is the final result of the planning process I will describe in this post: https://dicklesworthstone.github.io/beads_for_cass/ This functionality makes it very quick and easy to generate and deploy the exported site using the gh utility. The site itself usually consists of an sqlite file and a bunch of typescript and wasm that runs entirely in the browser, but with very good performance and nice features and styling, which you can observe in the example just given. Now, sharing MCP Agent Mail messages or a bunch of beads is one thing, but sharing a bunch of coding agent session logs is very different; these things are often full of sensitive information, API keys, curses/invective (at least mine are!), and other material that you definitely wouldn't want to expose to the world. But GitHub Pages, as nice as it is, only works for public repos (btw, my tools also support Cloudflare pages, but GH Pages is better and easier for this use case). So how to handle these problems? The answer is encryption: the user first selects which coding agents to include, which project folders, the time period, etc. and a bundle is generated (note that this bundle is in the canonical format that Cass internally converts all coding agent messages into from their original native formats) and then the user provides a password to use for the encryption of that bundle. So the idea is that although the repo and web page are public, everyone but you and others you tell the password to will simply see a password field and will be unable to read any of the messages. Once the password is entered, it would unlock a beautiful, responsive UI that lets you easily search across the messages nearly as quickly and efficiently as Cass does. And if you really have nothing to hide, you can leave the password off and make it all actually public. Anyway, this is a super complex new feature to add for many reasons. The export wizard alone is extremely intricate and complex. But the additional requirements around encryption and security make this hard to get right. Anyway, I asked Claude Code with Opus 4.5 to first study all the relevant code from my bv project for implementing this feature, and then explained how the corresponding feature in Cass would have to differ. It then created for me an initial cut at a plan. I asked CC to check over the bv code again for more insights and understanding into how the feature was implemented in bv and to then use those insights to expand and improve the plan document in-place. Finally, I moved the plan over to ChatGPT Pro in the browser with GPT 5.2 with Extended Reasoning along with this prompt preamble: "Carefully review this entire plan for me and come up with your best revisions in terms of better architecture, new features, changed features, etc. to make it better, more robust/reliable, more performant, more compelling/useful, etc. For each proposed change, give me your detailed analysis and rationale/justification for why it would make the project better along with the git-diff style change versus the original plan shown below:" I then pasted the outputs into Claude Code like this: OK, now integrate these revisions to the markdown plan in-place; use ultrathink and be meticulous. At the end, you can tell me which changes you wholeheartedly agree with, which you somewhat agree with, and which you disagree with: ```[Pasted text #1 +995 lines]``` I then rinsed and repeated this another 2 times. This is not a fast process: the last review, which you can see a screenshot of below, took 27 minutes to finish: You can see the final ~3,500-line markdown plan here: https://github.com/Dicklesworthstone/coding_agent_session_search/blob/main/PLAN_TO_CREATE_GH_PAGES_WEB_EXPORT_APP.md… But what's more interesting is to see the revisions in GitHub, where you can see the massive improvements that are made from v2 to v3, and how the improvements actually continue all the way to the end: This ended up taking a total of around 3 hours. DO NOT OVERSIMPLIFY THINGS! DO NOT LOSE ANY FEATURES OR FUNCTIONALITY! Also make sure that as part of the beads we include comprehensive unit tests and e2e test scripts with great, detailed logging so we can be sure that everything is working perfectly after implementation. I've found that you continue to get more and more improvements, even if they're subtle, the more times you run this in a row with Opus 4.5 (note that the following prompt is only for use AFTER you've already turned your initial markdown plan into beads using the other prompt I gave recently in my recent very long post about my workflows): "Reread AGENTS dot md so it's still fresh in your mind. Remember to ONLY use the `bd` tool to create and modify the beads and to add the dependencies to beads. Use ultrathink." I used to only run that once or twice before starting implementation, but I experimented recently with running it 6+ times, and it kept making useful refinements. If it starts to flatline in terms of incremental improvements to the beads, you might try starting a brand new CC session, starting it with: "First read ALL of the AGENTS dot md file and README dot md file super carefully and understand ALL of both! Then use your code investigation agent mode to fully understand the code, and technical architecture and purpose of the project. Use ultrathink." And then following up with the same prompt as shown above, but prefaced with: "We recently transformed a markdown plan file into a bunch of new beads. I want you to very carefully review and analyze these using `bd` and `bv`." The more complex and intricate your markdown plan is, the more relevant this technique is. If you have a small, trivial plan and a very simple project, this is obviously overkill. But in that case, you will likely see little in the way of incremental gains/changes with each round, so it should be fairly obvious when it's time to stop. Just remember: planning tokens are a lot fewer and cheaper than implementation tokens. Even a very big, complex markdown plan is shorter than a few substantive code files, let alone a whole project. And the models are far smarter when reasoning about a plan that is very detailed and fleshed out but still trivially small enough to easily fit within their context window (this is really the key insight behind my obsessive focus on planning and why I spent 80%+ of my time on that part). And if you lean on GPT Pro with Extended Reasoning in the web app for the initial planning as I strongly advocate (that is, to create and improve your markdown plan that you eventually turn into beads), you basically get those on an all-you-can-eat basis with a Pro plan, so take full advantage of that! No other model can touch Pro on the web when it's dealing with input that easily fits into its context window. It's truly unique. Now, you can still get a lot of extra mileage by blending in smart ideas from Gemini3 in the web app with Deep Think enabled, or from Grok4 Heavy, or Opus 4.5 in the web app, but you still want to use GPT Pro on the web as the final arbiter of what to take from which model and how to best integrate it.
